The vertical strip of fabric running down the front opening is called the placket, and gives a more symmetrical appearance to the joint between the left side, on top, and the right. This left over right order is also seen in waistcoat and coat fastenings, though women's clothing buttons the other way (right over left). The buttonholes, aligned vertically, are placed on the placket, though the top button and buttons at the bottom of stiff fronts are aligned horizontally. The buttonholes are one of the few places where the difference between hand and machine stitching can be observed while the shirt is being worn, and fashion designers sometimes use contrasting thread here or on the buttons themselves for extra impact.,           </p>,           <p>,             To give extra fullness to the back, there are often ,             <a href="/wiki/Pleat" title="Pleat">,               pleats,             </a>,              where the back panel joins to the yoke. On some fittings these are not needed, and handmade shirts may feature the extra fabric being worked continuously into the seam. In America, a box pleat is common (two pleats together in the centre), while in Britain the pleats are placed wider out under the shoulders.,           </p>,           <p>,             The less casual shirts in Britain will have no pockets, but the standard shirt in America has a single one on the wearer's left side, which is a sewn-on patch with a plain upper hem, optionally with a single button for closure. Less formal shirts may have small pockets on the sleeves as well. ,             <a href="/wiki/Shoulder_strap#Shirt_shoulder_strap" title="Shoulder strap">,               Shoulder straps,             </a>,              are virtually non-existent on formal shirts, with the exception of military clothing.,           </p>,           <p>,             Short-sleeved shirts have a plain (no-button) hem above the wearer's elbow. The natural fibers used in the past were ,             <a href="/wiki/Cotton" title="Cotton">,               cotton,             </a>,              (the most frequent), ,             <a href="/wiki/Linen" title="Linen">,               linen,             </a>,              (the oldest), ,             <a href="/wiki/Ramie" title="Ramie">,               ramie,             </a>,             , ,             <a href="/wiki/Wool" title="Wool">,               wool,             </a>,              or ,             <a href="/wiki/Silk" title="Silk">,               silk,             </a>,             . Silk is occasionally worn, though it is hot to wear and has a marked sheen.,           </p>,           <p>,             Yarns from these fibers are woven into a variety of different weaves, the most notable of which include ,             <a href="/wiki/Broadcloth" title="Broadcloth">,               broadcloth,             </a>,             , with double the number of ,             <a href="/wiki/Warp_(weaving)" title="Warp (weaving)">,               warp,             </a>,              to ,             <a href="/wiki/Weft" title="Weft">,               weft,             </a>,              threads, giving a smooth, formal shirting; ,             <a href="/wiki/Twill" title="Twill">,               twill,             </a>,             , where the tucks of the weft do not line up, giving a diagonal pattern, a weave used for most country checked (,             <i>,               e.g.,             </i>,             <a href="/wiki/Tattersall_(cloth)" title="Tattersall (cloth)">,               Tattersall,             </a>,             ) shirtings; ,             <a href="/wiki/Poplin" title="Poplin">,               poplin,             </a>,             , with a heavier warp than weft, giving more formal fabric; and ,             <a href="/wiki/Oxford_(cloth)" title="Oxford (cloth)">,               Oxford,             </a>,              weaves. Originally, in the ,             <a href="/wiki/Edwardian_era" title="Edwardian era">,               Edwardian era,             </a>,             , when the modern shirt emerged, all shirts were white. Herringbone patterns are worn informally and casually. Some colours, such as purple or pink, are generally only worn with city shirts. Further, the use of colour is seasonal, with shades like green being associated more with autumn than summer ones like yellow. The hem is tucked into the ,             <a href="/wiki/Trouser" title="Trouser" class="mw-redirect">,               trouser,             </a>,             . For ,             <a href="/wiki/Informal_(attire)" title="Informal (attire)" class="mw-redirect">,               informal-,             </a>,              or ,             <a href="/wiki/Formalwear" title="Formalwear" class="mw-redirect">,               formalwear,             </a>,             , a ,             <a href="/wiki/Coat_(clothing)" title="Coat (clothing)">,               coat,             </a>,              and ,             <a href="/wiki/Necktie" title="Necktie">,               tie,             </a>,              (or ,             <a href="/wiki/Bow_tie" title="Bow tie">,               bow tie,             </a>,             ) are compulsory. When a tie is worn, the top button of the shirt is fastened, so the tie can fit snugly around the wearers neck with a neat appearance.,           </p>,           <p>,             When a tie is not worn, conventions on buttoning differ globally: in the United States and the United Kingdom, the top button is virtually never buttoned if a tie is not worn  but unbuttoning two or more buttons is seen as overly casual. In France, unbuttoning two buttons is more common, and politicians appear on TV in this style.,           </p>,           <p>,             In casual usage, these conventions are often not followed, with many choosing to wear shirts not tucked in, or leaving the top button undone with a tie. This is commonly done by children and young men, particularly as part of ,             <a href="/wiki/School_uniform" title="School uniform">,               school uniform,             </a>,             , where it is not allowed. Even more casually, some now choose not to iron their shirts, or use nontraditional 'non-iron' fabrics.,           </p>,           <p>,             Similarly, as part of more casual work attire, some American men wear shirts with the top two buttons unbuttoned (buttoned at the third button), though buttoning at the fourth button is widely seen as too casual. Accordingly, some shirts are manufactured with a difference at the second or third button, by way of subtle cue as to where to button.,             <sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference">,               <a href="#cite_note-14">,                 <span>,                   [,                 </span>,                 14,                 <span>,                   ],                 </span>,               </a>,             </sup>,              Most casually, the shirt can be worn entirely unbuttoned, over a T-shirt.,           </p>,           <h2>,             <span class="mw-headline" id="Fit">,               Fit,             </span>,             <span class="mw-editsection">,               <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">,                 [,               </span>,               <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dress_shirt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fit">,                 edit,               </a>,               <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">,                 ],               </span>,             </span>,           </h2>,           <p>,             In the US, ,             <a href="/wiki/Ready-to-wear" title="Ready-to-wear">,               ready-to-wear,             </a>,             <a href="/wiki/Clothing_size" title="Clothing size" class="mw-redirect">,               sizes,             </a>,              of dress shirts traditionally consist of two numbers such as ,             <i>,               15½ 34,             </i>,             , meaning that the shirt has a neck 15.5 inches in girth (measured from centre of top button to centre of corresponding buttonhole) and a sleeve 34 inches long (measured from midpoint of the back and shoulders to the wrist). However, to reduce the number of sizes needed to be manufactured and stocked, an average sleeve length is sometimes given in the form ,             <i>,               15½ 34/35,             </i>,              (indicating a neck 15.5 inches in girth and a 35 inch sleeve). Since the cuff frequently features two buttons, the cuff diameter can be reduced so that the cuff does not come down over the hand, allowing the shirt to fit the shorter length. Since the sleeve and neck size do not take into account waist size, some shirts are cut wide to accommodate large belly sizes. Shirts cut for flat stomachs are usually labeled, "fitted", "tailored fit" "athletic fit" or "trim fit". The terms for fuller cut shirts are more varied ("Traditional", "Regular" etc.) and sometimes explained on a shirt maker's website. Additionally, "Portly" or "Big" are often used for neck sizes of 18 inches or more. Very casual button-front shirts are often sized as small, medium, large, and so on. The meaning of these ,             <i>,               ad-hoc,             </i>,              sizes is similarly not standardized and varies between manufacturers.,           </p>,           <p>,             In the ,             <a href="/wiki/Bespoke" title="Bespoke">,               bespoke,             </a>,              (custom-made) industry, where each shirt is made from an individually drafted pattern, these sizing problems are avoided, but there are still different ways making the shirt fit. While many choose to cut the sleeve long, and have the cuff catch on the hand to regulate its length, some prefer the much harder option of using a high armhole and carefully tailored shape, so that the cuff can be loose and still sit in exactly the right place wherever the arm moves.,             <sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference">,               <a href="#cite_note-15">,
