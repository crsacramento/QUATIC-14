          <p>
            The 
            <b>
              moka pot
            </b>
             is a stove-top 
            <a href="/wiki/Coffee_maker" title="Coffee maker" class="mw-redirect">
              coffee maker
            </a>
             which produces coffee by passing hot water pressurized by steam through ground 
            <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">
              coffee
            </a>
            . It was patented for the first time in 
            <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
              Italy
            </a>
             by the inventor Luigi De Ponti for 
            <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_Bialetti" title="Alfonso Bialetti">
              Alfonso Bialetti
            </a>
            , in 1933.
            <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
              [
              <i>
                <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
                  <span title="This is not common knowledge (October 2013)">
                    citation needed
                  </span>
                </a>
              </i>
              ]
            </sup>
            <i>
              Bialetti Industrie
            </i>
             continues to produce the same model under the name "Moka Express".
          </p>
          <p>
            The moka pot is most commonly used in 
            <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
              Europe
            </a>
             and in 
            <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
              Latin America
            </a>
            . It has become an 
            <a href="/wiki/Icon" title="Icon">
              iconic
            </a>
             design, displayed in modern 
            <a href="/wiki/Industrial_art" title="Industrial art" class="mw-redirect">
              industrial art
            </a>
             and 
            <a href="/wiki/Design_museum" title="Design museum">
              design museums
            </a>
             such as the 
            <a href="/wiki/Wolfsonian-FIU" title="Wolfsonian-FIU">
              Wolfsonian- FIU
            </a>
            , 
            <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Modern_Art" title="Museum of Modern Art">
              Museum of Modern Art
            </a>
            , the 
            <a href="/wiki/Cooper%E2%80%93Hewitt,_National_Design_Museum" title="Cooper–Hewitt, National Design Museum">
              Cooper–Hewitt, National Design Museum
            </a>
            , the 
            <a href="/wiki/Design_Museum" title="Design Museum">
              Design Museum
            </a>
            ,
            <sup id="cite_ref-HILARY_GREENBAUM_1-0" class="reference">
              <a href="#cite_note-HILARY_GREENBAUM-1">
                <span>
                  [
                </span>
                1
                <span>
                  ]
                </span>
              </a>
            </sup>
             and the 
            <a href="/wiki/Science_Museum_(London)" title="Science Museum (London)" class="mw-redirect">
              London Science Museum
            </a>
            . Moka pots come in different sizes, from one to eighteen 50 ml cups.
            <sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference">
              <a href="#cite_note-2">
                <span>
                  [
                </span>
                2
                <span>
                  ]
                </span>
              </a>
            </sup>
             The original design and many current models are made from 
            <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
              aluminium
            </a>
             with 
            <a href="/wiki/Bakelite" title="Bakelite">
              bakelite
            </a>
             handles.
          </p>

          <h2>
            <span class="mw-headline" id="Variations_and_brands">
              Variations and brands
            </span>
            <span class="mw-editsection">
              <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
                [
              </span>
              <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moka_pot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Variations and brands">
                edit
              </a>
              <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
                ]
              </span>
            </span>
          </h2>
          <div class="thumb tright">
            <div class="thumbinner" style="width:261px;">
              <a href="/wiki/File:Moka_Animation.gif" class="image">
                <img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/dd/Moka_Animation.gif" width="259" height="300" class="thumbimage" data-file-width="259" data-file-height="300"/>
              </a>
              <div class="thumbcaption">
                <div class="magnify">
                  <a href="/wiki/File:Moka_Animation.gif" class="internal" title="Enlarge">
                    <img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.24wmf7/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt=""/>
                  </a>
                </div>
                
processes of providing coffee by Moka pot
              </div>
            </div>
          </div>
          <p>
            Traditionally, moka pots are made of 
            <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
              aluminium
            </a>
             and are used over a flame or electric range. The aluminium moka pots cannot be used on 
            <a href="/wiki/Induction_cooker" title="Induction cooker" class="mw-redirect">
              induction stoves
            </a>
            . There are, however, also many stainless steel models available.
            <sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference">
              <a href="#cite_note-3">
                <span>
                  [
                </span>
                3
                <span>
                  ]
                </span>
              </a>
            </sup>
             Electric self-heating moka pots are also available. More recently induction specific moka pots are now available and are made of a titanium-alloy base.
            <sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference">
              <a href="#cite_note-4">
                <span>
                  [
                </span>
                4
                <span>
                  ]
                </span>
              </a>
            </sup>
          </p>
          <p>
            <i>
              Brikka
            </i>
             is a modified moka pot manufactured by Bialetti. It incorporates a weighted valve as a 
            <a href="/wiki/Pressure_regulator" title="Pressure regulator">
              pressure regulator
            </a>
             on top of the nozzle that allows pressure to build up inside the water tank in a manner similar to a 
            <a href="/wiki/Pressure_cooker" title="Pressure cooker" class="mw-redirect">
              pressure cooker
            </a>
            . As pressure builds up more quickly in this method (since there is much less leakage of vapor) compared to the standard moka pot, it reaches the level required for water to rise through the ground coffee at a lower temperature. However, the weighted valve allows pressure to accumulate and temperature to rise somewhat further before the liquid bursts through the nozzle. The result is coffee brewed at a higher pressure and temperature than the standard pot, making it more similar to 
            <a href="/wiki/Espresso" title="Espresso">
              espresso
            </a>
            , and therefore with more visible crema.
            <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
              [
              <i>
                <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
                  <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">
                    citation needed
                  </span>
                </a>
              </i>
              ]
            </sup>
          </p>
          <p>
            <i>
              Mukka Express
            </i>
             is a modified moka pot also manufactured by Bialetti that allows milk to be frothed and mixed with the coffee during brewing. The name, "Mukka", is a pun on the Italian for cow, 
            <i>
              "mucca"
            </i>
            . Bialetti also manufactures several stainless steel moka pots, e.g.: Musa, Class and Venus.
          </p>
          <p>
            <a href="/wiki/Alessi_(company)" title="Alessi (company)" class="mw-redirect">
              Alessi
            </a>
             is an Italian kitchenware manufacturer known for their moka pots. Cuisinox also markets several models of moka pots in both aluminium and stainless steel.
            <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
              [
              <i>
                <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
                  <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">
                    citation needed
                  </span>
                </a>
              </i>
              ]
            </sup>
          </p>
          <p>
            Also the high quality and design oriented Italian kitchenware manufacturer 
            <a href="/w/index.php?title=Serafino_Zani_(company)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Serafino Zani (company) (page does not exist)">
              Serafino Zani
            </a>
             is known for his moka pots: "Finlandia" designed by 
            <a href="/wiki/Tapio_Wirkkala" title="Tapio Wirkkala">
              Tapio Wirkkala
            </a>
            , "Mach" designed by 
            <a href="/w/index.php?title=Isao_Hosoe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Isao Hosoe (page does not exist)">
              Isao Hosoe
            </a>
             and awarded with the 
            <a href="/wiki/Good_Design_Award_(Chicago)" title="Good Design Award (Chicago)">
              Good Design Award (Chicago)
            </a>
             1993, "Thema" in stainess steel with 
            <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">
              titanium
            </a>
             and "Genesis" in stainless steel and 
            <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">
              copper
            </a>
            , both designed by Tarcisio Zani.
          </p>
          <p>
            Top Moka is an Italian manufacturer well known for their variety of different colors and their induction moka pot models.
            <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
              [
              <i>
                <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
                  <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">
                    citation needed
                  </span>
                </a>
              </i>
              ]
            </sup>
          </p>
          <p>
            Vev Viganò is an Italian manufacturer that specialises in stainless steel moka pots. Their product lines include Kontessa, Itaca, Vespress and Carioca.
            <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
              [
              <i>

          <h2>
            <span class="mw-headline" id="Brewing_coffee_with_a_moka_pot">
              Brewing coffee with a moka pot
            </span>
            <span class="mw-editsection">
              <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
                [
              </span>

          </h2>
          <div class="thumb tright">
            <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">

          <p>
            The boiler (marked A in the diagram) is filled with water almost up to the safety release valve and the 
            <a href="/wiki/Funnel" title="Funnel">
              funnel
            </a>
            -shaped metal filter (B) is inserted. Finely-ground 
            <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">
              coffee
            </a>
             is added to the filter as shown below. Then the upper part (C, which has a second metal filter at the bottom) is tightly screwed onto the base. The pot is placed on a suitable 
            <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">
              heat
            </a>
             source, the water is brought to its 
            <a href="/wiki/Boiling_point" title="Boiling point">
              boiling point
            </a>
            , and thereby 
            <a href="/wiki/Steam" title="Steam">
              steam
            </a>
             is created in the boiler.
          </p>
          <p>
            A 
            <a href="/wiki/Gasket" title="Gasket">
              gasket
            </a>
             ensures a tightly closed unit and allows for pressure to safely build up in the lower section, where a 
            <a href="/wiki/Safety_valve" title="Safety valve">
              safety valve
            </a>
             provides a necessary release in case this pressure should get too high (with clean filters, that should not happen).
          </p>
          <p>
            The steam eventually reaches a high enough 
            <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
              pressure
            </a>
             to gradually force the surrounding boiling water up the funnel through the coffee powder and into the upper chamber (C), where the coffee is collected. When the lower chamber is almost empty, steam bubbles mix with the upstreaming water, producing a characteristic gurgling noise. This "strombolian phase" allows a mixture of superheated steam and water to pass through the coffee, which leads to undesirable results, and therefore brewing is stopped as soon as this stage is reached.
            <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1359431108002299">
              [1]
            </a>
          </p>
          <h2>
            <span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance">
              Maintenance
            </span>
            <span class="mw-editsection">
              <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
                [
              </span>
              <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moka_pot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Maintenance">
                edit
              </a>
              <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
                ]
              </span>
            </span>
          </h2>
          <p>
            Moka pots require periodic replacement of the rubber seal and the filters, and a check that the safety release valve is not blocked.
          </p>
          <p>
            After use, a thin coat of oily coffee residue is left lining the interior of the stems, filters and upper chamber. It is said to be desirable to retain this residue, as it subsequently prevents coffee from acquiring an unpleasant metallic taste through contact with the aluminum wall.
            <sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference">
              <a href="#cite_note-9">
                <span>
                  [
                </span>
                9
                <span>
                  ]
                </span>
              </a>
            </sup>
          </p>

          <h2>
            <span class="mw-headline" id="Moka_coffee_vs._drip_coffee">
              Moka coffee vs. drip coffee
            </span>
            <span class="mw-editsection">
              <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
                [
              </span>
              <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moka_pot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Moka coffee vs. drip coffee">
                edit
              </a>
              <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
                ]
              </span>
            </span>
          </h2>
          <p>
            The flavor of Moka pot coffee depends greatly on bean variety, roast level, fineness of grind, and the level of heat used. Contrary to popular belief, although the "boiler" on a Moka pot is at elevated pressure and contains high-temperature steam, the water forced up through the grounds is no hotter (up to 90C, depending on the stage of extraction) than that used in other brewing methods.
            <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1359431108002299">
              [2]
            </a>
            <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://pubs.acs.org/doi/pdf/10.1021/jf070779x">
              [3]
            </a>
            . However the resultant brew does have increased extraction of 
            <a href="/wiki/Caffeine" title="Caffeine">
              caffeine
            </a>
             and flavors from the grounds versus filter coffee, resulting in a stronger brew than that obtained by 
            <a href="/wiki/Drip_brew" title="Drip brew">
              drip brewing
            </a>
            .
          </p>
          <h2>
            <span class="mw-headline" id="Moka_coffee_vs._espresso_coffee">
              Moka coffee vs. espresso coffee
            </span>
            <span class="mw-editsection">
              <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
                [
              </span>
              <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moka_pot&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Moka coffee vs. espresso coffee">
                edit
              </a>
              <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
                ]
              </span>
            </span>
          </h2>
          <p>
            Moka pots are sometimes referred to as stove-top espresso makers and produce coffee with an extraction ratio similar to (but somewhat higher than) that of a conventional 
            <a href="/wiki/Espresso_machine" title="Espresso machine">
              espresso machine
            </a>
            .
            <a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1359431108002299">
              [4]
            </a>
             Depending on bean variety and grind selection, Moka pots can create a foam emulsion, known as crema. However a typical Moka coffee is extracted at relatively low pressures of 1-2 
            <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">
              bar
            </a>
            ,
            <sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference">
              <a href="#cite_note-10">
                <span>
                  [
                </span>
                10
                <span>
                  ]
                </span>
              </a>
            </sup>
             while standards for espresso coffee specify a pressure of 9 bar.
            <sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference">
              <a href="#cite_note-11">
                <span>
                  [
                </span>
                11
                <span>
                  ]
                </span>
              </a>
            </sup>
            <sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference">
              <a href="#cite_note-12">
                <span>
                  [
                </span>
                12
                <span>
                  ]
                </span>
              </a>
            </sup>
             So, while a Moka coffee pot can produce a crema similar to espressos, different equipment is required to make a true espresso.
          </p>
